Listless

“I’m detransitioning,” Kay says for the fifth time that day.

“Okay, Kay,” I respond without looking up from my book. I smile a little to myself, appreciating the rhyme.

“No, I’m serious.” I sigh, arching my back and sitting up.

She doesn’t look serious; when Kay is serious, she looks pissed. Right now, she has a carefully neutral expression, which she tends to adopt when she’s trying not to cry.

“Lilith,” she whines. It’s taking me too long to respond.

“You’re not detransitioning. That would be cringe,” I offer, but her face doesn’t change. “It’s not a funny bit.”

While I scramble for something else to say, she stares down at her hands.

“What is this, a commercial for jeans?”

“Who is she?”

“She’s literally so slay.”

Mia lounges on the other half of the bench while I stare.

The object of our appreciation turns, scanning around for a moment until her gaze lands on us. I politely turn my head about 5 degrees to the right. I could conceivably be looking at a dog or a tree somewhere in the middle distance.

The stranger is far less polite than I.

“Hey! Enjoying the view?”

Who does that? Then again, I probably shouldn’t be ogling strangers in the park like some lech.

“Sorry! Your outfit is just really cute!”

Mia gives me a look.

“What? Here, give me a second.” Oh no. She’s coming over. I fight the urge to bury my head in my hands.

“Hi. I’m Brendan,” says Brendan, coming to a stop a respectful ten feet away.

I’m so fucking cancelled. I open my mouth, but Mia gets there first.

“Sorry Brendan, we thought you were a girl. You’re not, right?”

“No. Common mistake, I guess.”

“You sure? I’ve got some estrogen you could try.”

Mia is grinning like a maniac. Goddamnit.

“Thanks for the offer, but I’ve found it doesn’t agree with me.”

“What?”

“I’m a trans guy.”

I feel my face turning a bright red as Mia throws her head back and cackles.

It’s 12:14am, and I’m outside smoking from the bong. I can never sleep this early. My eyes, iridescent like a cat’s, stare out over the street from the landing outside my apartment building. No one is around.

No one is ever around when I feel like this.

I take another hit and pass the joint back. Staring at the sky, I ask, “Is there anything you miss about being a guy?” There are only a few stars visible, though earlier I saw a plane fly overhead. Now nothing moves.

“I miss dating straight women.”

I laugh and look over at her. “Yeah, like me.”

Kay frowns. “I’m serious, Lil.”

“You’re too serious. Remember what I said the other day? Being trans is just like, an inherently funny thing to do.”

“But I’m not trying to be funny!” She sits up. There’s a desperation in her voice, and it cracks a bit at the end of her sentence. I wince, and immediately regret it. The last thing this situation needs is secondhand voice dysphoria.

But she doesn’t seem to notice. Instead, she lies back and says, “I guess trying to be funny usually isn’t funny.”

“Exactly. We’re comediennes,” I shoot back, rolling over to put my arm on her stomach.

It doesn’t make sense for me to be this way. I flinch whenever Amber moves suddenly, expecting to get hit. I stare longingly at the ropes backstage at work. It’s not like anyone has ever hit me, or told me I should die — no one except me, I guess.

How’d I get this fucked up?

Inside it’s somehow 1:20am. I avoid eye contact with the mirror as I stand up, bending low over the sink to wash my hands. As I reach for the door handle, I finally think of something to say.

“So I was in the park the other day, and—”

Fuck. Amber’s asleep on the couch. I took too long. I look at my watch, but I don’t remember when I last checked it. It’s 11:04pm now, for whatever that’s worth.

I turn off the TV, which is paused in the middle of an episode of Twin Peaks, and poke the sleeping girl gently on the cheek. When that does nothing, I grab her shoulder and give it a shake.

“Huh?”

“You fell asleep on the couch. Come on, come get in bed.”
