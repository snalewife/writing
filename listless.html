Listless

“I’m detransitioning,” Kay says for the fifth time that day.

“Okay, Kay,” I respond without looking up from my book. I smile a little to myself, appreciating the rhyme.

“No, I’m serious.”

I sigh, arching my back and sitting up.

She doesn’t look serious; when Kay is serious, she looks pissed. Right now, she has a carefully neutral expression, which she often wears when she’s trying not to cry.

“Lilith,” she whines. It’s taking me too long to respond.

“You’re not detransitioning. That would be cringe,” I offer, but her face doesn’t change. “This isn’t a funny bit.”

While I scramble for something else to say, she stares down at her hands.

“What is this, a commercial for jeans?”

“Who is she?”

“She’s literally so slay.”

Kay lounges on the other half of the bench while I stare.

The object of our appreciation turns, scanning around for a moment until her gaze lands right on us. I politely turn my head about 5 degrees to the left. I could conceivably be looking at a dog or a tree somewhere in the middle distance.

The stranger is far less polite than I.

“Hey! Enjoying the view?”

Who does that? Then again, I probably shouldn’t be ogling people in the park like some lech.

“Sorry! Your outfit is just really cute!”

Kay gives me a look.

“What? Here, give me a second!” Oh no. She’s coming over. I fight the urge to bury my head in my hands.

“Hi. I’m Brayden,” says Brayden, coming to a stop a respectful ten feet away.

I’m so fucking cancelled. I open my mouth, but Kay gets there first.

“Sorry Brayden, we thought you were a girl. You’re not, right?”

“No. Common mistake, I guess.”

“You sure? I’ve got some estrogen you could try.” Kay is leaning forward and grinning like a maniac. Goddamnit.

“Thanks for the offer, but I’ve found it doesn’t agree with me.”

“What?”

“I’m a trans guy.”

I feel my face turning a bright red as Kay throws her head back and cackles.

It’s 12:14am, and I’m outside smoking from the bong. I can never sleep this early. My eyes, iridescent like a cat’s, stare out over the street from the landing outside my apartment building. No one is around.

No one is ever around when I feel like this.

Brayden’s wings are enormous. They’ve been modestly hidden beneath his oversized shirt, but when he takes it off to show us his scars — who does that? in a bar?? — they unfurl behind and above his head, resplendent and black-feathered.

I want to cry just looking at them.

As he lets them loose, a server walks behind him, and has to duck low over the tray of drinks she’s carrying. She shoots him a look, and he apologizes with the goofy charisma I’m learning is his trademark.

“Wow, those are crazy,” says Kay, looking him up and down.

“Which, the scars or the wings?” He asks, turning his radiant smile on her.

“Yes,” she replies, looking up from his abs to meet his playful gaze.

I’m surprised either of them notice a half hour later when I get up to pay my tab.

I take another hit and pass the joint back. Staring at the sky, I ask, “Is there anything you miss about being a guy?” There are only a few stars visible, though earlier I saw a plane fly overhead. Now, nothing moves.

“I miss dating straight women.”

I laugh and look over at her. “Yeah, like me.”

Kay frowns. “I’m serious, Lil.”

“You’re too serious. Remember what Elle said the other day? Being trans is just like, an inherently funny thing to do.”

“But I’m not trying to be funny!” She sits up. There’s a desperation in her voice, and it cracks a bit at the end of her sentence. I wince, and immediately regret it. The last thing this situation needs is secondhand voice dysphoria.

But she doesn’t seem to notice. Instead, she lies back and says, “I guess trying to be funny usually isn’t funny.”

“Exactly. We’re comediennes,” I shoot back, rolling over to put my arm on her stomach. I feel it rise and fall, and convince myself for a moment that I can feel the gentle suction there as she breathes through her skin.

I avoid eye contact with the mirror as I stand up, bending low over the sink to wash my hands. As I reach for the door handle, I finally think of something to say.

“So I was in the park the other day, and—”

Fuck. Amber’s asleep on the couch. I took too long.

I turn off the TV, which is paused in the middle of an episode of Twin Peaks, and poke her gently on the cheek. When that does nothing, I grab her shoulder and give it a shake.

“Huh?”

“You fell asleep on the couch. Come on, come get in bed.”
