She avoids eye contact with the mirror as she stands up, and bends low over the sink to wash her hands, as if not trusting her legs to keep her standing upright. As she reaches for the door handle, she finally thinks of something to say.

“So I was in the park the other day, and—”

Fuck. She has taken too long, and Amber is asleep on the couch. She looks at her watch, but doesn’t remember when she last checked it. It’s 11:04pm, for what it’s worth.

She turns off the TV, which is paused in the middle of an episode of Twin Peaks, and pokes her girlfriend gently on the cheek. When that does nothing, she grabs her shoulder and gives it a shake.

“Huh?”

“You fell asleep on the couch. Come on, come get in bed.”

It’s 12:14am, and she is outside smoking a joint. She can never sleep this early. From the landing outside her apartment building, just above street level, her eyes stare out, she imagines like a cat’s would. No one is around.

No one is ever around when she feels like this.

Unbidden, a memory rises up to the forefront of her thoughts; a constructed memory, for sure. She is young, just old enough to be in school, standing in a stairwell, holding a milk carton. On her way from one place to another, but she doesn’t know where. There is a window at the landing, and sunbeams are flooding in through it.

It must have been near Saint Patrick’s Day, because she feels certain she remembers leprechauns having something to do with all this. Someone had spilled milk somewhere, and said it was a leprechaun that got away?

This is straining the very limits of her recall. Time to go inside.

Inside it’s somehow 1:20am. She sits down hard on the armchair next to the couch, rocking it slightly. She probably opens her computer and closes it again about four times before she stands back up and goes into the kitchen, where she lists around for a minute before retrieving a bag of potato chips from the cupboard.

It’s 1:24am, and the bag of chips is empty. She feels like garbage, but what are you gonna do, right?

She thinks about killing herself in an idle way, as she has all her life. What if I just walked off this balcony? The knife is right there, what if I just sliced my wrists open instead of chopping onions?

What if I just walked off into the night and didn’t come back?

It didn’t make sense for her to be this way. She flinched whenever Amber moved suddenly, expecting to get hit. She stared longingly at the ropes backstage at work. It’s not like anyone had ever hit her, or told her she should die — no one except me, I guess.

How’d she get this fucked up?
