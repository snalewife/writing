Grey’s Astronomy

The human body is an incredibly complex system. We don’t entirely know how it works. There are mysteries around every corner.

The metal floor was cold under Meredith Grey’s bare feet. She shoved them into her slippers and stood up from her bunk.

“It’s definitely not time yet. Where are you going?” came a voice from behind her. She turned to gaze at Derek, whose face was pressed into his pillow. His salt-and-pepper hair was sticking up at a jaunty angle.

“I have to pee,” she whispered.

A spaceship is a lot like the human body. Cells rushing around from place to place, doing their jobs to keep us up and running. But there are no mysteries. Everything works the way it was designed to.

In the bathroom, she stared down at the water in the sink as it circled the drain. Then, she met her own eyes in the mirror.

“What are you looking at?”

Abruptly, she was on the floor. No, the wall. That was probably a bad noise her shoulder had made…

When she came to, she knew her shoulder was dislocated. She recognized the loose feeling and the dull, steady pain from when she had broken her toe on a doorframe when she was twelve.

She lay crumpled in a heap on the floor (actually the floor this time), with her busted arm pinned under her torso.

Until it doesn’t.

The common area of the engineers’ quarters was buzzing with activity. Chief Engineer Webber was shouting commands left and right.

“Alright people, you know the drill. Maintaining pressurization is the priority, and after that, life support systems. Shepherd, I want a full scan of the hull, pull whoever you need to place patches. Torres, Stevens, make sure our oxygen tanks are intact. Hunt, Bailey, Karev, check on the stiffs.”

Meredith’s arm was (un)comfortably dangling in a sling she had fashioned from a t-shirt. Derek gave her a kiss on the forehead as he hustled past, heading for the aft control station.

Cristina entered the common area and looked around. Meredith avoided eye contact. She felt bad that she had been avoiding her best friend, but that didn’t stop her from doing it.

It was a silly impulse. Obviously Cristina saw her, and when Meredith glanced up she saw the other woman making a beeline for her.

“What happened to you? Nevermind, don’t answer that. Wanna see something cool?”

Meredith opened her mouth, closed it again, and thought for a moment.

“What?”

Cristina grabbed her by the hand and led her out of the room at a trot.

They turned down hallway after hallway until at last they arrived at the thruster control room. Or, arrived at the outside of it.

Where there was normally an open, hexagonal doorway into the room, a heavy blast door now stood. There was a small square of reinforced glass in the center of it, and Cristina led her right up to it, then pointed wordlessly at the window.

The thruster control room was unrecognizable. One of the consoles was missing entirely, and there were shredded wires and warped sheets of metal floating roughly where it had stood. And there was a big rock sticking through the floor.

Meredith looked at Cristina, who turned to meet her eyes with a gleeful expression on her face. Over Cristina’s shoulder, she noticed a compartment on the wall that was uncharacteristically lying open. Inside was a rack of compression suits with matching helmets, intended for performing external repairs to the ship.

Cristina followed her gaze and nodded excitedly. Then, they both turned to look back through the window.

Bailey was already calling up the schematics for the suspension pods on her overlay as she bustled down the corridor. Karev jogged beside her like a pilot fish following a shark around. Hunt had already disappeared around a corner at a sprint.

Bailey was mid-sentence, explaining her thought process for Karev’s benefit. “Now, we can do a lot from the computer controls, but if there’s something wrong with the pods themselves, what do we do?”

Karev gave her a blank look. She sighed.

“If there’s something wrong with the pods, we’re gonna need a coolant guy.”

She tried not to think about what that might mean for her, personally.

They turned the corner into the control room for the suspension pods and saw Hunt already bent over a console, his orange hair glistening with sweat.

Bailey immediately got to work at the other console, still mentally rotating the schematic for the pods while subvocalizing status report commands to be executed by the console.

Glancing over, she noticed that Hunt was cursing under his breath, and had the front plate off of his console, exposing the electronics underneath. He already had a screwdriver in hand, but would need more tools if he was about to dig around in there. Silently, Bailey motioned to Karev to bring a toolbox from the supply closet around the corner.

From there, she started to get lost in the work. There was a comfortable rhythm to dispatching a command, scanning through the reports that came back for any signs that something was out of the ordinary, and verifying visually that the relevant system seemed to be functioning in the nearest pod.

She had almost forgotten that she had sent Karev out of the room until she heard a loud smacking noise to her left, and looked over to see Hunt holding his hand and Karev reeling from what was likely a punch.

“What on Earth are you doing, Engineer Hunt?” She exclaimed furiously. They’d had some problems in the past, but this was a bridge too far.

“You startled me,” mumbled Hunt.

“Karev, are you okay?” Bailey was too upset to multitask, momentarily abandoning her status reports.

“Fuck,” said Karev, spitting out some blood onto the metal floor.

“Apologize,” Bailey said through gritted teeth. She had noticed how quickly Hunt had gone back to what he was doing, without even thanking Karev for the toolbox.

“No,” Hunt said without looking up. “Don’t sneak up on people, Karev.”

Bailey was momentarily at a loss for words, doing her best not to be visibly taken aback. However, this was no time for an interpersonal conflict.

“People punching people, what is that about?” she muttered, returning to her work.

Karev had recovered by now, and went off into the long room with the suspension pods.

“Um, guys?” his voice came through the doorway.

Bailey dropped what she was doing and ran in there with him. She saw immediately what he was looking at. The third pod from the end on the right hand side had a streak of liquid running down the inside of its transparent front. The pods were at an angle, and as they watched, a drop of liquid fell onto the nose of the man inside.

Bailey looked at Karev. “I think we need a coolant guy,” he said.

Lexi liked robots. They were like people, but unlike people, they did what you wanted them to. Most of the time.

Right now, the autoforklift was giving her a world of trouble. It had been in use during the abrupt deceleration, and had collided rather unfortunately with another machine. Since then, she’d run every diagnostic in the system menu, and it was still misbehaving.

This time wouldn’t be any different, she knew. She’d just finished re-flashing the machine’s firmware, but all she’d done was add a bunch of logging statements everywhere. If there was going to be an error message, surely she would see it.

“Tell me your secrets, you jerk,” she mumbled, patting the thing’s stout chassis. As she queued up the test program on her overlay, however, a ruggedly masculine voice startled her.

“I hear you catch more flies with honey, honey.” Mark was standing in the doorway with a charming, if irritating, grin splitting his broad face wide open.

Lexi absentmindedly tucked a strand of hair behind her ear. “You shouldn’t sneak up on people. It’s — it’s rude.”

This was so not what she needed right now.

“I’m not rude. I’m a perfect gentleman.” Mark cocked his head to one side, and the stupid grin intensified.

“Whatever. Why are you here?”

“I heard about your friend there and thought you could use some help. You figure out what’s wrong with it yet?”

Lexi scowled. “No. Every time, it’s the same thing, he just freaks out—”

“He? Lexi, come on. I was joking when I called it your friend. You know they’re not people, right?”

She felt her cheeks turning red and abruptly turned away from him. “Obviously. I know that. You just — after working on them for a while — I mean, each machine has a personality, in a way — I know they’re not people.” Her face was hot with embarrassment.

“Hey, chill out. It’s cute. Hell, I like to give them names. Some of them, at least.”

Lexi turned back around hesitantly, only to find Mark much closer than she expected. He was inspecting the manual controls on the autoforklift, which happened to be right next to her face.

Seeing the machine, she was reminded of her frustration. “We should name this one ‘Jerky McJerkface’.” Mark turned to look at her with a playfully puzzled expression. “Or, um, something funnier than that,” she rushed out, blushing even more.

The splicer rotated gently in the air — no, the space — in front of Meredith’s face. Distantly, she noted its movement, and registered vaguely that she ought to be repairing the seam between the metal sheet she was magnetically stuck to and the one next to it.

“Mer?” came Cristina’s distracted voice over the communicator, a voice she loved. See? She could still love.

“Hm?”

“What have you been up to? I feel like we haven’t talked in a while.”

“Oh.” Right. She had definitely noticed. Meredith took a deep breath. “Yeah, sorry. I’ve just been so busy with this project the Chief has me and Derek working on.”

“Right, right. I should have a project,” Cristina said absentmindedly. “How is Derek?”

“What? Cristina, what is this?”

Her friend turned her head around in her spacesuit, and when she saw the expression on Meredith’s face, turned the rest of her body around too.

“What do you mean, what? Is something wrong?”

“No, we just don’t have to always talk about everything!”

“Is this about me taking credit for fixing the overlay thing? Because that wasn’t my fault, Bailey just said good job and then I couldn’t correct her because the conversation moved on.”

“Cristina.”

“What?”

Meredith gave her a meaningful glance.

“Oh my god you’re all dark and stormy again? But I thought things were good, you said you were doing good!”

“I lied.”

“I’m your person, Meredith. Or, I mean, I am your person, right?”

“Of course, what am I gonna do, talk to Derek?”

“This isn’t a joke!”

She finally turned her head away from the seam to see a look of genuine hurt on Cristina’s face.

“Cristina —”

She was cut off by the squeaky sound of warping metal. The two women turned their heads in unison to look, then turned to look back at each other. Meredith opened her mouth and turned back as if to say more, but was interrupted by an even louder squeal.

“Come on, help me with this!” Cristina was already firing the thrusters on her suit downward to stabilize the bent panel of the ships hull between them, and welding with the hand that wasn’t keeping her anchored to a ladder rung.

Meredith looked around her in a way that was totally not despondent, and activated her own suit’s thrusters.
